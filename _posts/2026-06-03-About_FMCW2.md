> **Part 2** of the FMCW notes. Part 1 ended at the **double-FFT**; here we look at the same chirp train as
> **one single FFT**, and at what sets the resolution.

<br>

## Part 1 in a Nutshell

1. Transmit a linear chirp; the echo returns delayed by $t_d = \dfrac{2R}{c}$.
2. Mixing Tx × Rx and low-pass filtering leaves the **beat** signal; for a still target its frequency gives range, $R = \dfrac{c\, f_{\text{beat}}}{2 B f_r}$.
3. A **moving** target adds Doppler: the per-chirp beat frequency is $f_{I,n} = \dfrac{2V}{c}f_c + B f_r t_0 + \dfrac{2VBn}{c}$.
4. **One chirp → range only**; the **phase change across many chirps → velocity**.
5. **Double-FFT**: FFT *within* a chirp = range, FFT *across* chirps = velocity → a range-Doppler map.

<br>
<hr>

## Single-FFT: One Transform Over the Whole Chirp Train

Instead of two transforms, take **one** Fourier transform over the *entire* received signal — all $N$ chirps.
Split the time axis into per-chirp windows: chirp $n$ occupies

$$t \in \left[\,nT_r - \tfrac{T_r}{2},\; nT_r + \tfrac{T_r}{2}\,\right] = \left[\,(2n-1)\tfrac{T_r}{2},\; (2n+1)\tfrac{T_r}{2}\,\right]$$

The chirp-$n$ **beat voltage** is $V_{I,n}(t) = A\cos\big[\phi_{I,n}(t_i)\big]$, where $t_i$ is the time
within the chirp and the phase (from Part 1) lists out as

$$\phi_{I,n}(t_i) = \phi_0 \;-\; 2\pi f_c\frac{2V}{c}\,nT_r \;-\; 2\pi\underbrace{\Big[\tfrac{2V}{c}f_c + B f_r t_0 + \tfrac{2VBn}{c}\Big]}_{f_{I,n}}t_i$$

The single Fourier transform — **the integral over time that turns voltage-vs-time into voltage-vs-frequency** —
is the sum of the windowed chirps:

$$V_I(f) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \int_{(2n-1)T_r/2}^{(2n+1)T_r/2} V_{I,n}(t)\, e^{-i2\pi f t}\, dt = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} V_{I,n}(f)\, e^{-i2\pi f n T_r}$$

The factor $e^{-i2\pi f n T_r}$ is **not optional**. Substituting $t = nT_r + t_i$ pulls each chirp's time slot
out of the integral (Fourier **shift theorem**):

$$\int_{(2n-1)T_r/2}^{(2n+1)T_r/2} V_{I,n}(t)\, e^{-i2\pi f t}\, dt = e^{-i2\pi f n T_r}\underbrace{\int_{-T_r/2}^{T_r/2} A\cos[\phi_{I,n}(t_i)]\, e^{-i2\pi f t_i}\, dt_i}_{V_{I,n}(f)\;=\;\text{sinc peaked at } f_{I,n}}$$

So each chirp contributes its sinc $V_{I,n}(f)$ **times** the slot phase $e^{-i2\pi f n T_r}$, and that
cross-chirp phase is what stacks up into the Doppler dimension. (Both $V_{I,n}(t)$ and $V_{I,n}(f)$ are the
*same* beat voltage — the FFT just re-expresses it from time to frequency.)

This is a **different processing method**, not the double-FFT folded up — one transform instead of two. It is
simple, but it behaves very differently for stationary vs moving targets.

<br>
<hr>

## Stationary vs Moving in a Single FFT

- **Stationary target** — its beat frequency is the *same* in every chirp, so all $N$ windows line up and add
  **coherently** at one bin → a single **tall, sharp peak** (high voltage).
- **Moving target** — its beat frequency **drifts** chirp to chirp (Doppler + range walk), so the peaks land on
  *different* bins and never line up. The energy **spreads** into a low, **continuous** band instead of a peak.

![Single-FFT response: stationary target gives a sharp peak, moving target spreads out](single_fft_static_vs_moving.png)
_In a single FFT, a stationary target peaks sharply (all energy in one bin) while a moving target's energy smears across many bins — low and continuous._

That smearing is exactly **why the double-FFT method exists**: it transforms across chirps separately, putting
each moving target's drift onto its own Doppler axis and re-concentrating it into one sharp range-Doppler cell.

## Summing the Chirps: Array Factor & Resolution

Now actually evaluate the sum. Near the peak, every chirp's transform is the same sinc envelope $K(f)$ times its
slow-time Doppler phase $e^{+i2\pi f_d nT_r}$ (with $f_d = \tfrac{2V}{c}f_c$), so

$$V_I(f) = K(f)\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} e^{-i2\pi(f - f_d)\,nT_r}$$

That sum is a geometric series — the **Dirichlet kernel**, the exact same *array factor* as an $N$-element
antenna array:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} e^{-i2\pi(f-f_d)nT_r} = \frac{\sin\!\big[2\pi(f-f_d)\tfrac{NT_r}{2}\big]}{\sin\!\big[2\pi(f-f_d)\tfrac{T_r}{2}\big]}\; e^{-i2\pi(f-f_d)\frac{(N-1)T_r}{2}}$$

So the single FFT **factorizes** into single-chirp envelope × array factor:

$$\boxed{\;V_I(f) = K(f)\,\frac{\sin\!\big[2\pi(f-f_d)\tfrac{NT_r}{2}\big]}{\sin\!\big[2\pi(f-f_d)\tfrac{T_r}{2}\big]}\; e^{-i2\pi(f-f_d)\frac{(N-1)T_r}{2}},\qquad f_d = \frac{2V}{c}f_c\;}$$

Every exponential here is a **unit phasor in the complex plane** — $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ with
$|e^{i\theta}| = 1$. So the trailing $e^{-i2\pi(f-f_d)(N-1)T_r/2}$ only **rotates the phase**; the spectral
*magnitude* $|V_I(f)|$ comes entirely from $K(f)$ and the $\sin/\sin$ ratio.

- $K(f)$ — the **single-chirp sinc envelope**, peaked at the beat $f_{I,n}\approx f_d + B f_r t_0$ → it fixes *which* band holds the energy, i.e. **range**.
- the **array factor** — a *periodic comb* with teeth every $1/T_r$, the whole pattern offset by the Doppler $f_d$; its main-lobe width $\approx \dfrac{1}{NT_r}$ sets the **velocity resolution**.

So $|V_I(f)|$ actually peaks at the **full beat $f_{I,n}$** (range *and* Doppler): the envelope $K(f)$ selects the comb tooth nearest the range beat, and that tooth's sub-$1/T_r$ offset is the Doppler → velocity. The Doppler is the comb's **fine offset**, not a lone peak at $f_d$.

The array factor's main-lobe width is the **velocity (Doppler) resolution**:

$$\boxed{\;\Delta f = \frac{1}{N\,T_r}\;}$$

As $N \to \infty$ the array factor sharpens toward a spike at $f = f_d$ (the discrete sum approaching a
continuous transform — a Riemann sum): more chirps (longer observation $T_\text{obs} = N T_r$) → finer
**velocity** resolution. **Range** resolution, by contrast, is set by the sweep bandwidth
($\Delta R = \tfrac{c}{2B}$ from Part 1) — bandwidth controls range, observation time controls velocity. The
Dirichlet kernel's **sidelobes** are the price, suppressed later by **windowing / weighting** the chirps.

This sharp, coherent peak assumes **negligible range walk** (validity condition ③ below); once a target's range
walk over the CPI exceeds a cell, the peak **smears** instead — exactly the moving-target spreading shown earlier.

## Parameters & Validity Conditions

A quick reference for the quantities above ($T_c = N T_r$ is the total observation / coherent processing interval):

| Symbol | Meaning | Formula |
|--------|---------|---------|
| $N$ | number of chirps | $N = \dfrac{T_c}{T_r}$ |
| $f_{DM}$ | max Doppler frequency | $f_{DM} = \dfrac{2V_m}{c}f_c$ |
| $\Delta R$ | range resolution | $\dfrac{c}{2B}$ — set by **bandwidth** |
| $\Delta V$ | velocity resolution | $\dfrac{c}{2 f_c N T_r}$ — set by **observation time** $T_c$ |
| $V_m$ | max unambiguous velocity | $\dfrac{c}{4 f_c T_r}$ — Nyquist $f_r \ge 2 f_{DM}$ |

**When is the single-FFT / array-factor picture valid?** The approximations (linear phase, one sharp Doppler
peak, negligible range walk) need:

1. $\dfrac{2V_m}{c} \ll 1$ — non-relativistic targets ($V \ll c$).
2. $N \gg 1$ — enough chirps for the Dirichlet kernel to act like a sharp sinc.
3. $B\,\dfrac{2V_m}{c}\,T_r N \ll 1$ — range walk over the whole CPI stays within one range cell.
4. $\dfrac{2V_m}{c} f_c T_r \le \dfrac12$ — equivalently $f_{DM}\,T_r \le \tfrac12$, the **Nyquist** limit (Doppler stays unambiguous); this is exactly what fixes $V_m = \dfrac{c}{4 f_c T_r}$ in the table above.

## Summary

1. **Single-FFT** = one transform over the whole chirp train = $\displaystyle V_I(f) = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} V_{I,n}(f)\, e^{-i2\pi f n T_r}$ (each chirp windowed to $[(2n-1)\tfrac{T_r}{2},\,(2n+1)\tfrac{T_r}{2}]$; the slot phase $e^{-i2\pi f nT_r}$ from the shift theorem).
2. It's a **different method** from the double-FFT: a **stationary** target gives one tall sharp peak, but a **moving** target's beat drifts chirp-to-chirp so its energy **smears** across bins (low, continuous) — which is why the double-FFT is preferred when targets move.
3. Summing the chirps yields a **Dirichlet kernel / array factor** $\dfrac{\sin[2\pi(f-f_d)NT_r/2]}{\sin[2\pi(f-f_d)T_r/2]}$ peaked at the Doppler $f_d = \dfrac{2V}{c}f_c$, so $V_I(f) = K(f)\times(\text{array factor})$ = single-chirp sinc (**range**) × sharp Doppler peak (**velocity**).
4. Frequency resolution is $\Delta f = \dfrac{1}{N T_r}$ (the array-factor main-lobe width) — more chirps (longer observation) → finer velocity resolution; range resolution stays $\dfrac{c}{2B}$.
5. Reference: $N=\dfrac{T_c}{T_r}$, $f_{DM}=\dfrac{2V_m}{c}f_c$, $\Delta R=\dfrac{c}{2B}$, $\Delta V=\dfrac{c}{2f_c N T_r}$, $V_m=\dfrac{c}{4f_c T_r}$ — valid when $V\ll c$, $N\gg1$, and range walk over the CPI stays sub-cell.
